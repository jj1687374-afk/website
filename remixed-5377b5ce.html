Walk into South Asian Bazaar on Morse Road on any given afternoon and you'll hear it before you see it — the quiet sound of gold being worked. Tucked inside Columbus's most beloved South Asian marketplace, Bhabi Lal Gold House has become something rare in an era of mass-produced accessories: a genuine goldsmith's atelier, right in the middle of Ohio.

The operation belongs to Prince Rasaily, whose family carries decades of Nepali jewelry-making tradition. What started as a destination for Columbus's South Asian community has quietly become one of the most trusted names in 24-karat gold jewelry in the entire state.

"People come to us because they know exactly what they're getting," Rasaily says. "Real gold. Real craft. We don't cut corners."

A Craft Carried Across Continents

Traditional Nepali goldsmithing is a discipline that cannot be rushed. Each piece — from a delicate tilhari necklace to a ceremonial bangle set — is formed by hand, shaped, textured, and finished without the shortcuts that define most modern jewelry retail. At Bhabi Lal Gold House, that tradition is non-negotiable.

Hand-formed 24K gold pieces — each one made to last a lifetime.

The pieces are striking in their weight and warmth. Unlike 14K or 18K gold jewelry common in American jewelry chains, Bhabi Lal specializes in 24-karat — the purest form, the standard for traditional South Asian bridal and ceremonial jewelry. It is gold the way it has always been worn across Nepal and India: bold, meaningful, and built to be passed down.

Silver work is available too. Rasaily and his team can execute virtually any design — whether a customer brings in a reference photo, a family heirloom for repair, or simply a vision.

"We totally handmake every piece. If you want any type of Nepali or Indian jewelry, let us know — we can do it."

— Prince Rasaily, Proprietor

More Than a Store — A Community Landmark

Columbus has one of the fastest-growing Nepali communities in the Midwest. For many families, Bhabi Lal Gold House is the first call for bridal jewelry, the trusted name for repairs, and the place you go when a piece needs to actually mean something. The store draws customers from across Ohio and beyond — and ships nationally and internationally for those who can't make the trip.

The workshop inside South Asian Bazaar on Columbus's Morse Road.

Rasaily has built his reputation quietly — word of mouth, community trust, and a Facebook presence that shows the work rather than performing it. Videos on their YouTube channel offer an unfiltered look at the craft: gold being shaped, pieces being finished, customers leaving with something they'll wear for the rest of their lives.

Trading is also part of the business. Customers can bring in old gold — whether jewelry that no longer fits, broken pieces, or inherited items — and exchange for new work. It's an arrangement that reflects how gold is treated in South Asian culture: not a disposable accessory, but a living asset.

What They Make

The range at Bhabi Lal covers the full spectrum of traditional Nepali and Indian jewelry: tilharis, mangalsutra, churaa, haars, kangan, tops, and custom bridal sets. Repairs cover everything from broken clasps to full restorations. The team also works in silver — rings, earrings, and traditional pieces that call for the lighter metal.

For customers outside Columbus, the process is simple: reach out by phone, describe or send photos of what you need, arrange shipping, and receive the finished piece. Rasaily has customers across the United States and internationally who have never set foot in the store but return for every major jewelry purchase.

"In a city still discovering its South Asian roots, Bhabi Lal Gold House is already an institution."
